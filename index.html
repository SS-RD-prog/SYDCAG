About the Name

Why SYDCAG, not RNSCAG?

Sydney

The name reflects both the local foundation and the broader collaborative ambition of the programme. The initial focus is to formalise a structured approach to complex aortic care at Royal North Shore Hospital, supporting its role as a specialised hub within the Northern Sydney Local Health District. Beyond this local foundation, the "Sydney" component reflects an aspiration to create a collaborative forum with other experienced cardiovascular surgical teams across Sydney, centred on shared expertise, selected case discussion, academic exchange, and future research partnerships.

Complex

Because this is not intended to encompass all aortic work. The focus is on selected high-complexity cases requiring coordinated input from both vascular and cardiac surgery, supported by appropriate infrastructure, logistics, and resource allocation.

Aortic Group

Because this is not yet a centre or institute. "Group" reflects the current stage of development while allowing the programme to mature and scale over time.

Background

Why Now?

Royal North Shore Hospital already has the surgical expertise, the case volume, and the infrastructure. What it currently lacks is a formalised structure that brings these elements together consistently. Internationally, the shift toward dedicated aortic teams has accelerated — driven by guideline mandates, volume–outcome data, and the recognition that no single specialty commands the full breadth of complex aortic care alone. The gap between what RNSH can offer informally and what a structured programme would deliver systematically is the opportunity SYDCAG is designed to close.

Clinical Reference

SYDCAG Clinical Reference — Key Concepts & Thresholds

For the SYDCAG programme hub · Royal North Shore Hospital · 2026. Data summary of relevant current information to May 2026.

ACC / AHA 2022 — Aortic Disease Guideline (Isselbacher et al., Circulation, 2022)
Outcomes for patients with aortic disease are enhanced at programmes with higher volumes, experienced practitioners, and extensive management capabilities. The guideline emphasises the importance of institutional interventional volume and multidisciplinary aortic team expertise in determining the appropriate timing of intervention.
The most commonly cited features of a Multidisciplinary Aortic Team (MAT) include: cardiac surgical, vascular surgical, and endovascular specialists with extensive experience managing complex aortic disease at a centre with high aortic intervention volume; imaging specialists with expertise in CT, MRI, and echocardiography; anaesthesiologists experienced in the management of acute aortic disease; and intensivists, nurses, and coordinators committed to the longitudinal care of the aortic patient.
At centres with Multidisciplinary Aortic Teams and experienced surgeons, the threshold for surgical intervention for sporadic aortic root and ascending aortic aneurysms has been lowered from 5.5 cm to 5.0 cm in selected patients. A formally constituted MAT does not only improve process — it changes what is clinically possible.
The multidisciplinary aortic team is highly encouraged to involve the patient in decision-making, especially when individuals are on the borderline of thresholds for repair or eligible for different types of surgical repair.
EACTS / STS 2024 — Guidelines for Acute and Chronic Syndromes of the Aortic Organ (Czerny et al., 2024)
Shared decision-making for the optimal treatment of aortic pathologies by a multidisciplinary aortic team is recommended (Class I, Level C). In patients with multisegmental aortic disease, treatment is recommended in aortic centres providing open and endovascular cardiac and vascular surgery on site (Class I, Level C). Transfer to an aortic centre should be considered for patients with complex aortic pathologies (Class IIa, Level B).
The guidelines emphasise the importance of forming specialised teams for aortic diseases — recommending joint decisions with an aortic MDT (Class IC), referral of patients with multisegmental or complex aortopathy to specialised centres (Class IIa), and availability of a hybrid operating room for endovascular procedures.
ESVS 2024 — Clinical Practice Guidelines on Abdominal Aortoiliac Artery Aneurysms
The ESVS 2024 guidelines set a centre volume recommendation of ≥30 AAA repairs per year (with at least 15 open and 15 endovascular), and ≥20 complex aneurysm cases per year (combined open and fenestrated/branched EVAR) at specialised centres.

The relationship between volume and outcomes in complex aortic surgery is one of the most consistent findings in the cardiovascular surgical literature.

The volume–outcome evidence
A systematic review and meta-analysis of 79,131 patients across 30 observational studies found that patients treated in high-volume centres or by high-volume surgeons were associated with lower mortality rates (OR 0.51, 95% CI 0.46–0.56, and OR 0.41, 95% CI 0.25–0.66, respectively). Patients treated in centres that introduced a specific multidisciplinary aortic programme and a dedicated on-call aortic team also showed a significant reduction in mortality (OR 0.31, 95% CI 0.19–0.51, and OR 0.37, 95% CI 0.15–0.87, respectively). — Mariscalco et al., Eur J Prev Cardiol, 2018. All studies were observational; findings are associational, not causal.
High-volume centres demonstrate substantially lower mortality for emergency thoracic aortic dissection repair: 12.6% versus 20.6% at medium-volume and 23.9% at low-volume centres in multivariate analysis. Patients at low-volume centres were more than twice as likely to die compared to those at high-volume centres (OR 2.06, 95% CI 1.25–3.38). — Frontiers

When outcomes of acute Type A aortic dissection repair were specifically assessed six years after programme implementation, results showed a dramatic 10-fold reduction in operative mortality compared to the preceding era, despite only a modest increase in institutional volume.

What the evidence consistently shows
  • Formal aortic team programmes are associated with reduced operative mortality across multiple independent institutions and countries
  • The evidence base is observational — no randomised trials exist; findings are associational, not causal
  • Guideline endorsement is now clear and consistent across ACC/AHA, EACTS/STS, ESVS, and EACTS/ESVS joint consensus documents
  • No single specialty is uniformly expert in all aspects of complex aortic care — the interdisciplinary model exists because the anatomy requires it
What remains uncertain
  • Exact numeric volume thresholds for most complex thoracic procedures (Type A dissection, arch repair, TAAA)
  • The relative contributions of surgeon volume, institutional volume, team structure, and protocolisation to outcomes
  • Whether specific volume thresholds should be mandated versus used as quality benchmarks
Clinical bottom line: The weight of evidence supports concentrating complex aortic surgery in structured, high-volume programmes with formal multidisciplinary governance. The SYDCAG programme is RNSH's contribution to that standard.
The 14-day threshold: Current classification of Type B dissection as "acute" (<14 days) vs "subacute/chronic" (≥14 days) is a convention, not a validated therapeutic cutoff. The biological transition — aortic remodelling, false lumen thrombosis, wall compliance — is continuous and does not reset at day 14. TEVAR outcomes data do not demonstrate a discrete inflection at this threshold.

Clinical Pathway

Acute Aortic Response Protocol

A draft protocol for structured, simultaneous activation of both Vascular Surgery and Cardiothoracic Surgery at RNSH for complex acute aortic presentations. Prepared for interdisciplinary review — not yet approved for clinical use.

⚠ DRAFT — FOR INTERNAL REVIEW ONLY — NOT FOR CLINICAL USE
This protocol is a proposal prepared for interdisciplinary discussion between Cardiothoracic Surgery and Vascular Surgery at RNSH. Nothing in this document should be communicated to clinical areas, referring hospitals, or hospital administration until both departments have reviewed and agreed on the final content.
Background

This document proposes a draft Acute Aortic Response Protocol for SYDCAG — the collaborative aortic initiative between Cardiothoracic Surgery (CTS) and Vascular Surgery (VS) at RNSH. It is intended as a starting point for discussion, not a final document.

The protocol has two components: a concise reference card for display in clinical areas, and a supporting document setting out the rationale, activation criteria, and operational detail.

Purpose

The acute pathway is the component of SYDCAG with the most immediate clinical impact and the highest stakes if not clearly defined. At present, the response to a complex acute aortic presentation at RNSH depends on informal communication between whoever is on call.

A written protocol does not require excessive complexity — it needs to be explicit about who activates it, what triggers it, who gets called, and what happens next.

The initial framework draws on evidence showing improved outcomes when both open surgical and endovascular expertise are simultaneously available from the moment of diagnosis, and on previously described successful acute care pathway models.

☎
Single Activation

One call by the first assessing clinician — Emergency Physician, Medical Registrar, ICU, or any surgical team — triggers simultaneous notification of both SYDCAG specialty delegates.

📷
Imaging Standard

CT aortography (chest / abdomen / pelvis, contrast, arterial phase) is the default study. Point-of-care echo should be discussed as part of the protocol as an alternative for haemodynamically unstable patients before they can get a CT — functioning as an adjunctive diagnostic tool that, despite not replacing CT for operative planning, may have an important role in the activation algorithm. CT result communicated directly to both SYDCAG delegates simultaneously.

📥
Transfer Policy

RNSH accepts all complex acute aortic referrals from within the Northern Sydney Local Health District and other referring hospitals. No referral should be declined. Ambiguity about acceptance creates harmful delays in time-critical conditions.

Zone 3 / arch threshold rationale: Aortic compromise proximal to zone 3 requires surgical decision-making spanning both specialties — balancing open arch repair options against endovascular strategies for each individual risk profile and anatomical consideration. This mirrors the logic described in the Canadian framework (Can J Cardiol, 2023) and the 2024 EACTS/STS guideline definition of when a full aortic team is essential.
Activate
Type A Aortic Dissection (TAAD)

Primary activation for any TAAD not limited to the ascending aorta (arch or distal extension present).
Secondary activation (by CTS) if limited to the ascending aorta but with high-risk features or other considerations that prompt Vascular Surgery involvement, as determined by the CTS team.

Activate
Complicated Type B Dissection (TBAD)

Primary activation for any complicated TBAD (malperfusion, haemodynamic instability, rapid expansion, or rupture) with arch involvement (< zone 3).
Secondary activation (by VS) if zone 3+ with any high-risk feature or other considerations, as determined by the Vascular team, that prompts Cardiothoracic Surgery involvement.

Activate
Acute Aortic Syndrome — Zone 1 to Zone 3 with High-Risk Features

Primary activation for any zone 1–3 AAS (AD, IMH, PAU) with high-risk features such as haemodynamic instability, rapid expansion, severe aortic dilatation (>40 mm), or extensive periaortic haematoma.
(Specific criteria to be confirmed after CTS and VS discussion.)

Activate
Thoracic / Thoracoabdominal Aortic Rupture & Special Circumstances

Primary activation for any thoracic or thoracoabdominal aortic rupture (regardless of dissection or aneurysm-related).
Secondary activation for complicated aortic dissection requiring emergency intervention in special circumstances: high-risk patients, pregnant women, suspected connective tissue disorder, supraaortic or visceral branch compromise, or undetermined primary tear zone.
(Additional criteria as per multidisciplinary discussion.)

Activation Mechanism (Draft): Single activation by the first assessing clinician. Business hours: one call initiates simultaneous notification of both SYDCAG VS and CTS delegates for on-site review. After hours: simultaneous activation of both teams; on-site assessment led by the primary specialty (CTS for TAAD, VS for TBAD), with collaborative decision-making before any final operative plan. Contact method and on-call delegate roster to be confirmed.
